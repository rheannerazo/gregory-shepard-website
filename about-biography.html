The Beginning

Born owing
the world nothing.

An Rh incompatibility, nine blood transfusions, and a mother who refused to let go.

Gregory was born with an Rh blood incompatibility — a condition that, at the time, was poorly understood. He received nine blood transfusions as an infant, stopped breathing more than once, and was, at one point, declared dead. His older brother, born with the same incompatibility, did not survive.

His mother kept telling him a single sentence that became the through-line of everything: "You are here for a reason."

1970s–1980s · The Gauntlet

Stupid, slow, disruptive —
every label except the right ones.

School was its own kind of war. Bullied. Held back. Labeled stupid and lazy. What looked like defiance was actually seven undiagnosed neurodivergent conditions — autism, dyslexia, savant syndrome, synesthesia, and three more — quietly running an operating system the school system had no language for.

Gregory moved through four continuation schools. By the last one, he set himself his first real challenge — not a hike, not a mountain, just a question: can I finish this year? He did. Graduated in 1987. The first quest was over — he had no idea it had also started something else.

1994–2015 · The Builds

Thirteen companies.
Twelve exits. One playbook.

Every industry different. Every exit teaching the same lesson — quietly accumulating the pattern that would eventually become the seven phases.

1994 · First Build

Co-founded American Federal Lending — a direct lender to real estate, aggregator-focused rather than retail. Bought out within the year. First company, first exit, first lesson.

Late 1990s · Internet Pioneer

Travel insurance comparison sites. The first online passport and visa service. A currency exchange business. A bicycle headlamp company selling 250,000 units monthly. Each sold within three to six years.

2001–2015 · The Breakout

AffiliateTraction — the world's first affiliate marketing agency, first to go global, first to pivot into SaaS. Acquired by eBay Enterprise in a $925M cross-brand deal that won four PE Awards. AdAssured and Pepperjam followed from the same engine.

The Work

Doing the right thing
at the right time.

Most startups don't fail because founders lack intelligence, ambition, or effort. They fail because they apply the wrong strategies at the wrong stage — raising before they're ready, scaling before validation, hiring before operational clarity.

After building and selling twelve companies, Gregory began identifying a consistent pattern: successful founders weren't necessarily smarter or better funded. They were simply more likely to do the right thing at the right time. That idea became the foundation of everything since — the books, the lectures, the workshops, and the Startup Lifecycle framework.
